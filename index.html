Exhibit A

Passport
No. 2734078

Anatomy of Freedom in Post-War Europe

This document is more than an ID. It is a chronicle of a continent rising from the ruins.

Owner: Else Schmidt.
Profession: Housewife.
Issued: January 28, 1953.

Identity

Mother & Son

Frankfurt am Main, 1953. Note the metal rivets securing the photo—a hallmark of Cold War-era documentation.

Included in the passport is her 10-year-old son, Klaus-Peter (born 1943). Together, they stand on the threshold of a new Europe.

Barriers

US Occupation Zone

March 1953. Travel is not yet free. To cross into Austria, Else requires special clearance.

"Permit to enter the US Zone of Austria"

This stamp is an echo of the war. Salzburg remains under the authority of the Allied High Commission.

The Thaw

Dolce Vita

1955–1957. The borders begin to open. The stamps become chaotic, overlapping, vibrant.

ENTRATA — USCITA — RESIA

Else and Klaus travel south to Italy and Switzerland. The era of mass tourism begins. Borders turn into mere lines on a map, rather than walls.

Epilogue

Growing Up

Berlin, 1958. The final significant entry.

"Kind Klaus-Peter... gestrichen"

Klaus turns 15. He is crossed out from his mother's document to receive his own passport. One story ends, another begins.
